In 1890, the University of Chicago's campus aimed to “remove the mind of the student from the busy mercantile conditions of Chicago." Architect Henry Ives Cobbs envisioned a campus that would consist of one central quadrangle surrounded by six others, spanning 22 acres of land.
Today, the University currently or formerly has control over 200 properties in Hyde Park and the surrounding neighborhoods, spanning 65th Street to 38th Street and Lake Michigan to I-90.
Over its 130-year history, the University has come to control a substantial portion of properties in Hyde Park, serving as the primary agent in reshaping and transforming the neighborhood.
How the University of Chicago Created a “Compatible Community”
Mapping over a century of the University of Chicago’s property footprint in Hyde Park and surrounding neighborhoods
Written by Celeste Alcalay, Austin Steinhart, and Nikhil Patel
Design and Development by Austin Steinhart and Nikhil Patel

Conflicting priorities, land use disputes, and racial tension have driven the University of Chicago’s physical expansion and the evolution of a historically fraught “town and gown” relationship with Hyde Park and the surrounding neighborhoods. The University was the first higher education institution to embark on an urban renewal campaign of its kind, setting the stage for others to follow, a topic scholars and students have written on extensively.

Drawing from the University of Chicago plans, campus maps, academic literature, and other archival material, the Maroon has charted the University’s property acquisition from its charter in 1890 to the present.

The following narration is not an exhaustive history; rather, it traces the contours of shifting values and priorities that contributed to the institutional identity the University of Chicago holds today.

Methodology

This project drew data from several sources, including official University publications, campus maps, municipal records, satellite imagery, and news articles. Most building footprints were obtained from OpenStreetMap, a crowdsourced geospatial data repository; some historic building footprints were recreated from historic maps using Felt. Many dates of building ownership were sourced from the UChicago Heritage Map, opening announcements, and university press releases.
