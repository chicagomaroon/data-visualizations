The UChicago Endowment, Explained

What is it? Why should you care? And what do we know about it, exactly?
Written by Emily Sun and Karen Yi
Data and development by Karen Yi
December 1, 2025

In the past two years, several high-profile events have roiled campuses nationwide and caused increased scrutiny on university endowments. In 2023, campus organizers pressed for divestment from Israeli companies in response to the genocide in Gaza. Starting with President Trump's inauguration in January, once-reliable federal funding has become uncertain, causing many to believe universities should draw from their endowments to make up the loss. More generally, since elite universities overhauled their investment strategies starting around 1985, endowments have ballooned to controversial levels.

It seems like everyone suddenly has an opinion about endowments. But for the less Booth-inclined, this piece aims to answer: what is an endowment, exactly? How does it work? Who controls it? And why should we care? Answering these questions is not easy for the University of Chicago, because private universities have minimal reporting requirements and are accountable mainly to their stakeholders rather than to the public at large. Still, the beginnings of a picture can be developed from what little information exists.

The Maroon's data desk compiled datasets from three publicly available sources (XXX do we list what sources) to build the fullest picture to date of the University's endowment, which is one of the 20 largest private university endowments in the United States.

I. What is an endowment?

University endowments are separate from student tuition. That's because universities' income consists of many sources with different restrictions. Tuition goes towards day-to-day operating expenses without getting invested; endowments, on the other hand, stem from private donors and are managed by investors.

Universities withdraw a percentage from this endowment to cover their remaining costs after tuition and other revenue are accounted for, about 4-5% per year. The remainder of the endowment is re-invested. In 2024, the University withdrew 5% of its endowment to support operating costs.

Most of a university's endowment is typically restricted, which means that donors have put rules around what their donation can be used for. For example, the Pearson family moved to withdraw their funds from the Pearson Institute at UChicago due to alleged contract breaches in 2018.

X% of UChicago's endowment consists of restricted funds. Still, well-endowed universities don't have their hands completely tied. "They should make clear that their formidable financial endowments are not there to simply be envied or admired," wrote Larry H. Summers earlier this year. "Part of their function is to be drawn down in the face of emergencies, and covering federal funding lapses surely counts as one. Believe me, a former president of Harvard, when I say that ways can be found in an emergency to deploy even parts of the endowment that have been earmarked by their donors for other uses."

At UChicago, donations are funneled into a pot called the Total Return Investment Pool, or TRIP. The TRIP is in turn invested into various equities and assets. The major distinction here is between public equities--what most people think of as "stocks"--and private equities, which involve individual donors, venture capitalists, and hedge funds. Because stock exchanges are regulated by the federal government, more data is available on public equities compared to private equities.

II. What do we know about the endowment?

UChicago's endowment was valued at $10.4 billion in 2024, the equivalent of 1.3 years' worth of revenue and one of the largest in the country (CITE).

As for what the endowment is invested in… That's a little harder to see.

UChicago is a private university. This means that while it must comply with general financial reporting requirements for nonprofits, it is not subject to the more stringent requirements for public universities. It has incentive to keep all this behind closed doors: information about investment strategies would give institutions a competitive edge over their peers. This is one reason why universities have in the past refused to comply with activists' demands for transparency, although the University has on some occasions fulfilled narrower requests. When the Associated Press asked 50 public and private universities to provide information about their endowments, 39 schools refused and 4 did not respond. Even Congress has failed to glean information about endowments.

Still, even for a private university, UChicago is exceptional: in 2023, Amnesty International rated the University as the least transparent among 10 peer institutions, with a score of 0 out of 40. The University appears to have stopped publishing even its Annual Report since 2022.

All universities do have some information available, if one knows where to look.

III. Who controls the endowment?

For decades, campus groups wishing to influence the University's finances have held protests targeting either the Board of Trustees or the University President, both to little success. To understand why organizers target these two groups, it may help to take a look at the flowchart below.

As this chart demonstrates, the Board of Trustees is ultimately the final decisionmaker about investment approaches. The Board of Trustees acts as a steering committee for the Office of Investments, which is responsible for overseeing the endowment and answers to the President. The campus community has three avenues for giving input to investment strategy: the Faculty Senate, a board comprising 51 elected faculty; the Graduate Council, with 6 executive members and elected representatives from the 12 graduate divisions; and Advisory Councils, which have 10-95 members across 14 committees composed of "friends and alumni" of the University. None of these bodies currently have regular meetings with the Board of Trustees, although the Graduate Council held a quarterly "Student Perspectives Series" with the Board from 2016-2021.

The Office of Investments hires and communicates with external asset management firms. "Often, even the institutions themselves don’t know where their money goes, in part because they hire outside managers who constantly buy and sell investments. Additionally, many schools put their money into mutual or hedge funds, which in turn make other investments that can be kept confidential," reported the Associated Press in 2016.

But ignorance is a choice, not a rule. According to Brenden O'Connell, an employee of a hedge-fund firm that manages university endowments, any institution with a separately managed account (SMA) can request restrictions on their investments. "An SMA would be customized for you, usually. So you can impose constraints, [such as] you don't want exposure to XYZ industries, and then the manager will tailor the portfolio accordingly". For example, UChicago could easily request that its endowment is not invested in fossil-fuel companies. Harvard did exactly this in 2021.

Similarly, former Trustee Tom Cole appeared to deflect responsibility for decisions regarding investments. Notes from a 2016 meeting summarized his remarks: "It would be unusual for a discussion of a potential Kalven exception to emerge from within the Board itself. Rather, it is the role of the president and the officers to transmit issues of concern to the trustees and that discussions subsequently occur at the Executive Committee level and then might come before the entire Board."

Yet according to the official governing documents of the University of Chicago, as of May 2024, the President is actually "responsible, under the supervision of the Board, for the management of…investments, investment properties, and special property holdings in the neighborhood of the University" (emphasis added for clarity). In a 2015 interview, then-President Zimmer conceded: "Investments are the responsibility of the Board of Trustees…this is a Board matter, but if I had to report on my sense of the Board’s view of this—is that they’re unlikely to want to do that."

There is only one actor in the chart with greater or equal power to the Board: if donors specify that their donation cannot be used for a particular purpose, then the Office of Investments must comply.

IV. Why should we care?

Many members of campus have given statements on why they think greater transparency is needed. Below is a summary of some of their arguments.

The argument for fiscal responsibility

Even before the funding cuts, Professor Clifford Ando has dedicated ten op-eds in the Maroon to criticizing what he sees as the University's irresponsible management of funds, including growing debt and a disproportionately increasing presidential salary.

He calls for greater transparency and communication: "The University of Chicago’s pathology for secrecy regarding the simple facts of its own operations does not suggest confidence on the part of its leadership, despite their power. They should announce their vision proudly and provide the data for it to be analyzed and discussed."

The push for transparency is not limited to UChicago. Professors at UC CITE and students at the University of Michigan have also mounted campaigns to achieve accountability for school finances. QUOTE FROM THESE ARTICLES

The argument for moral responsibility

When it comes to investment, one important distinction is between the primary and secondary market. When a company goes public, it signals to the public that it needs to raise money amounting to its initial public offering (IPO). This is called the primary market, which typically happens only once to kickstart a burgeoning company. What most people think of as the stock market is actually the secondary market, where players on the stock market buy and trade stocks with each other. The key here is that the original company does not obtain direct monetary benefit from the secondary market. Therefore, "investing in" a company does not financially benefit that company.

Still, pro-divestment activists argue that investing in a company sends a message to the rest of the world, saying that the company's products are valuable. Withdrawing investments in a company, in turn, can pressure it to change its practices.

The argument for social responsibility

Most financial experts do agree that excluding any sector from a portfolio is not the most profitable strategy. Just like diversity in ecosystems or human societies is better for everyone, diversity in investment portfolios produces maximal return. Another way of putting it is the age-old saying: don't put all your eggs in one basket. Yet, again, fears about divestment may be overblown. Actually, O'Connell points out that the mere idea of "maximal return" is controversial even in the financial community. "So overall, let's say they they have a billion dollars, and then they generate $100 million profit, then they take $50 million and use that for spending to cover all sorts of operations, and then they have maybe $10 or $30 million of that to cover inflation, but then you still have, then you still have 30 left over. And some people will say that is unnecessary, because the endowment will keep that 30 and they'll reinvest it back into the portfolio, and it'll compound over decades…Some people will say that 30 million you didn't need, now you're just compounding the principal and the endowment's getting bigger and bigger." The way he describes this pattern brings to mind an ego contest between elite universities.

As endowments grow continually, some are questioning whether their original purpose as safety nets is still being honored.

Today's enormous endowments stem from the so-called Yale investment strategy, popularized by David Swensen in 1985. In a departure from the strategies of the time, Swensen posited that riskier investments--in private equity and international stocks--would yield higher returns. This change in strategy is reflected in the data about UChicago, which has invested increasingly in private equity over the last 20 years.
