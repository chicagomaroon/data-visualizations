The UChicago Endowment, Explained

What is it? Why should you care? And what do we know about it, exactly?
Written by Emily Sun and Karen Yi
Data and development by Karen Yi
February 1, 2026

In recent years, protests and funding cuts have roiled campuses nationwide and brought increased scrutiny to university endowments. From campus organizers pressing administrations to divest from Israeli companies, to arguments that universities should make up federal funding cuts using endowment money, to accusations of general mismanagement, observers are wondering what exactly is being done with the billions of dollars that universities rely on to fund research and education.

For the less Booth-inclined, this piece aims to demystify what endowments are, who manages them, and why they are important. Answering these questions about the University of Chicago's $10.9 billion endowment is not easy. Unlike public institutions, private universities have few reporting requirements and are accountable to their stakeholders, rather than to the public at large. The Maroon's data desk has developed the beginnings of a picture from what little information is accessible.

Our explainer draws from three publicly available sources: Form 13F from the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), Form 990-T from the Internal Revenue Service (IRS), and university reports. Form 13F is filed quarterly by institutions that manage over $100 million in assets; form 990 is filed annually by tax-exempt organizations. In addition to these data, the university commissions an annual financial report, which we supplemented with information from financial data sources accessed through the university library. Altogether, they form the fullest picture of the endowment, compiled by students, to date.

I. What is an endowment?

The endowment is just one of many sources that the University draws from to keep the lights on. Income for the University consists mainly of tuition, government grants, and the endowment. Tuition is collected from students and goes directly towards day-to-day operating expenses; the endowment, on the other hand, stems from private donors and is invested mainly as a rainy-day fund.

After tuition and other revenue are accounted for, universities withdraw an allowance from the endowment to cover remaining costs, amounting to about 4-6% per year. The remainder of the endowment is re-invested.

Most of a university's endowment is usually restricted, which means that donors have put rules around what their donation can be used for and can punish the school for violating those agreements. For example, the Pearson family moved to withdraw their funds from UChicago due to alleged contract breaches in 2018.

About two-thirds of UChicago's endowment consists of restricted funds. Still, well-endowed universities don't have their hands completely tied. Former Harvard president Larry H. Summers wrote earlier this year that in emergencies such as sudden loss of federal funding, even restricted funds can be used creatively.

At the University, the endowment is merged into one pot called the Total Return Investment Pool (TRIP). The TRIP is in turn invested into various equities and assets. The major distinction here is between public equities—commonly known as "stocks"—and private equities, which involve individual donors, venture capitalists, and hedge funds. Because stock exchanges are regulated by the federal government, more data is available on public stocks compared to private equities.

II. What do we know is in the endowment?

The University of Chicago has one of the 20 largest private university endowments in the United States, valued at $10.9 billion in 2025, yet one of the smallest among its peers.

As for what the endowment is invested in… That's a little harder to see.

UChicago is a private university. This means that while it must comply with general reporting requirements for nonprofits, it is not subject to the more stringent requirements for public universities. It has reason to keep its secrets behind closed doors: information about investment strategies would give institutions a competitive edge over their peers. This is one reason why universities have in the past refused to comply with demands for transparency, although the University has on some occasions fulfilled narrower requests.

Still, even for a private university, UChicago is exceptional: in 2023, Amnesty International rated the University as the least transparent among ten peer institutions on categories such as responsiveness to human rights, transparency in public or published records, and evidence of ESG (environmental-social governance) investing.

All universities do have some information available, if one knows where to look.

Public equities (stocks)

The federal Securities and Exchanges Commission requires all entities with significant investments to report on the amounts and names of stocks traded on public U.S. exchanges.

To provide information to donors and other interested parties, the University maintains some self-reporting on its finances.

Data compiled from the past twenty years of financial reports reveals that the University increased the proportion of the endowment invested in private equity to 35%, accompanied by a decrease in bonds. This strategy is consistent with the so-called Yale investment strategy, popularized by David Swensen in 1985. In a departure from prevailing strategies of the time, Swensen posited that riskier investments—in private equity and international stocks—would yield higher returns. III. Who controls the endowment?

The Board of Trustees acts as a steering committee for the Office of Investments, an entity within the President's office responsible for overseeing the endowment. The campus community has three avenues for giving input to investment strategy: the Faculty Senate, a board comprising 51 elected faculty; the Graduate Council, with 6 executive members and elected representatives from the 12 graduate divisions; and Advisory Councils, which have 10-95 members across 14 committees composed of "friends and alumni" of the University. None of these bodies appear to currently have regular meetings with the Board of Trustees, although the Graduate Council held a quarterly Student Perspectives Series" with the Board from 2016-2021.

The Office of Investments directly manages some of the endowment, but hires external asset management firms to administer the rest. "Often, even the institutions themselves don't know where their money goes, in part because they hire outside managers who constantly buy and sell investments. Additionally, many schools put their money into mutual or hedge funds, which in turn make other investments that can be kept confidential," reported the Associated Press in 2016. It is not clear from the available information to what extent this is true of the University.

But ignorance is a choice, not a rule. According to Brenden O'Connell, an employee of a hedge-fund firm that manages university endowments, any institution with a separately managed account (SMA) can request restrictions on their investments. "An SMA would be customized for you, usually. So you can impose constraints, [such as] you don't want exposure to XYZ industries, and then the manager will tailor the portfolio accordingly". For example, UChicago could request that its endowment is not invested in fossil-fuel companies, as Harvard did in 2021.

So who gets the final say? In a 2016 meeting with pro-divestment activists, former Trustee Tom Cole appeared to deflect responsibility for endowment strategy, saying that the responsibility for setting investment goals lies with the President's office.

Yet according to the official governing documents of the University of Chicago, as of May 2024, the President is "responsible, under the supervision of the Board, for the management of…investments, investment properties, and special property holdings in the neighborhood of the University" (emphasis added for clarity). In a 2015 interview, then-President Zimmer conceded: "Investments are the responsibility of the Board of Trustees."

There is only one player in the chart with greater or equal power to the Board: if donors mark any funds as restricted—in other words, specify that their donation can only be used for a particular purpose—then the Office of Investments must comply.

Conflicts of interest

The federal Internal Revenue Service requires all nonprofits to list financial conflicts of interest in their tax filings. The Maroon's review of these documents revealed that the University of Chicago has been hiring trustee-led asset management firms to manage its endowment.

In 2015, when the University nominated Man Group's CEO Emmanuel Roman to its Board of Trustees, Man Group was paid $992,000 for managing the endowment. (While the University may have worked with Man Group prior to this year, any such work would not have counted as a conflict of interest and was therefore not reported.) One year after Roman relocated to Pacific Investment Management Company (PIMCO), the University stopped employing Man Group and began employing PIMCO. It is unknown whether Roman is a member of the Board's Investment Committee, which determines whether he has direct influence over the endowment.

The University employed three other companies associated with trustees in similar capacities, despite having a policy of avoiding conflicts of interest with current trustees. Overall, the University has paid at least one investment firm with a conflict of interest every year since 2014. In 2024, the University stopped specific amounts paid to firms with conflicts of interest.

IRS data does not, however, provide any indication about how much of UChicago's endowment is invested with these firms and funds.

IV. Why should we care?

Many members of campus have given statements on why they think greater transparency is needed. Below is a summary of some of their arguments.

The argument for moral responsibility

When it comes to investment, one important distinction is between the primary and secondary markets. When a company goes public, it signals to the public that it needs to raise some amount of money, as indicated by its initial public offering (IPO). This is called the primary market, which typically happens only once to kickstart a burgeoning company. What most people think of as the stock market is actually the secondary market, where players on the stock market buy and trade stocks with each other. The key here is that the original company does not profit directly from the secondary market. For example, if the Maroon sold one share of Apple stock to Paul Alivisatos, Apple would not receive any proceeds from that transaction. Therefore, "investing in" a company does not financially benefit that company.

Still, pro-divestment activists argue that investing in a company sends a message to the rest of the world, saying that the company's products are valuable. The value of a company's stock relates to its reputation with customers and potential third-party investors. Withdrawing investments from a company, in turn, can pressure it to change its practices.

The argument for public responsibility

The push for transparency is not limited to UChicago. Many from both inside and outside campus have argued that it is the public's right to understand the influence that elite institutions wield with their purses, especially if those institutions receive federal funding. In 2012, Berkeley students expressed alarm after discovering that the University of California system was invested in Bushmaster, a manufacturer of assault-style rifles. A 2018 campaign at the University of Michigan resulted in stricter transparency measures for their equivalent of the Board of Trustees' Investment Committee, such as written conflict-of-interest disclosures. Even Congress has become involved, with both Democratic and Republican members unsuccessfully drafting legislation that would have required universities to be more accountable with their investments.

Some have also proposed alternative solutions for democratizing the endowment, such as letting students elect trustees, have direct representation on the board, or manage small portions of the endowment.

The argument for fiscal responsibility

Even before the funding cuts, Professor Clifford Ando has dedicated ten op-eds in the Maroon to criticizing what he sees as the University's irresponsible management of funds, including a large budget deficit in recent years, resulting in departmental losses and administrative departures. The deficit was finally reduced by half due to recent measures in fiscal year 2025.

He calls for greater transparency and communication: "The University of Chicago's pathology for secrecy regarding the simple facts of its own operations does not suggest confidence on the part of its leadership, despite their power. They should announce their vision proudly and provide the data for it to be analyzed and discussed."

If the goal of the endowment is to ensure financial stability for the university, it might not seem like a good idea to limit investment prospects. Most financial experts do agree that excluding any sector from a portfolio is not the most profitable strategy. Just like diversity in ecosystems or human societies is better for everyone, diversity in investment portfolios produces maximal return. In other words: don't put all your eggs in one basket. Yet, some firms argue that the financially responsible thing to do would be to avoid including fossil fuels in an investment portfolio, given the volatility of non-renewable resources. Such arguments point towards a need for institutions to take proactive approaches to investments, rather than staying "neutral" as the University of Chicago is wont to do.

Actually, O'Connell points out that the mere idea of "maximal return" is controversial. He gives a theoretical example of a $1 billion endowment: if a university invests the full amount and receives $100 million in profit, it might use about half of the profit to cover operating expenses such as professors' salaries or student programming. Another $10 million or so is lost to inflation, leaving $30 million in excess. When universities reinvest this excess rather than spending on students or communities in need, "some people say that $30 million in pure profit is unnecessary," says O'Connell. "It just makes the endowment bigger and bigger." The way he describes this pattern brings to mind an ego contest between elite universities.

As endowments continue growing amid federal cuts to university budgets, some are questioning whether their original purpose as safety nets is being neglected. "When these endowments are like, 'No, we're not going to step in here', then people are like, 'What's the point of the endowment?' Isn't this the exact scenario where the endowment should step in?" asks O'Connell.

Graph describing using SEC 13-F data: Stock holdings from the September 30 2025 13-F filing are included in this graphic. For index funds VOO, VT, and IVV, of which UChicago had 62,572, 239,037, and 195,789 shares respectively as of September 30 2025, the individual holdings and percent makeup of these funds were accessed using the Vanguard and IShares websites between July 29, 2025 and January 6, 2026. About 99.8%, 91.1%, and 99.9% of the constituent funds were identified, respectively. UChicago's shares in these companies were found by multiplying the percent makeup of each holding with respect to its parent fund by the dollar value of the parent fund's shares as reported in UChicago's September 30, 2025 13-F filing. The associated industry sector for each holding was accessed using Yahoo Finance on January 6, 2026. International stocks do not have data in Yahoo Finance, so we only considered sector data for domestic holdings, which make up 50% of all UChicago SEC holdings by dollar amount. The top five holdings per sector were selected by taking the five companies for which UChicago had the most holdings that year.

Chart describing conflicts of interest from 990 data: All statements in IRS Form 990, Schedule L, Section V from 2014 to 2022 that mentioned "university's investment" were extracted and parsed for organization, trustee, relationship, and amount paid. (2013 was the first year that Schedule L was included in Form 990.)
