In 1890, the University of Chicago's newly chartered campus aimed to “remove the mind of the student from the busy mercantile conditions of Chicago." Architect Henry Ives Cobbs envisioned a campus with one central quadrangle surrounded by six others, spanning 22 acres of land.
Today, the University currently or formerly has control over 300 properties in Hyde Park and the surrounding neighborhoods, spanning 38th Street to 65th Street and I-90 to Lake Michigan.
A Neighborhood Institution:
How the University of Chicago Built a “Compatible Community”
Mapping over a century of the University of Chicago’s expanding property footprint in Hyde Park and the surrounding neighborhoods.
Written by Celeste Alcalay, Nikhil Patel, and Austin Steinhart
Design and Development by Austin Steinhart
Data Compiled by Nikhil Patel, Celeste Alcalay, and Austin Steinhart

Conflicting priorities, land use disputes, and racial tension have driven the University of Chicago’s physical expansion and the evolution of a historically fraught “town and gown” relationship with Hyde Park and the surrounding neighborhoods. Setting the stage for others to follow, the University was the first higher education institution to embark on an urban renewal campaign of its kind, a topic University scholars and students have written on extensively.

Drawing from the University of Chicago building blueprints, campus maps, academic literature, and other archival material, the Maroon charted the University’s property acquisition from its 1890 charter to the present. (The University maintains a map of campus properties. The Maroon requested a copy of this map, which the University declined to provide.)

The following narration is not an exhaustive history; rather, it traces the contours of shifting values and priorities that contributed to the University of Chicago’s current institutional identity.

▸ Read about our Methodology

This project drew data from several sources, including official University publications, campus maps, municipal records, satellite imagery, and news articles. Most building footprints were obtained from OpenStreetMap, a crowdsourced geospatial data repository; some historic building footprints were recreated from historic maps using Felt. Many dates of building ownership were sourced from the UChicago Heritage Map, opening announcements, and university press releases. Maps are sourced from the UChicago Library’s Map Collection, the University of Chicago Photographic Archive, and from Racial Restrictive Covenants on Chicago's South Side in 1947.
